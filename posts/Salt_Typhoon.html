The APT has infiltrated organizations with advanced cybersecurity protocols and managed to
bypass detection systems that are specifically designed to identify intrusions of this nature.
Security experts believe this is due to their use of custom malware, advanced obfuscation
techniques, and a deep understanding of U.S. cybersecurity defense mechanisms. It is believed
that the group still has access to a majority of U.S. telecommunications companies, and it’s
proving extremely difficult to remove them.
2021 Microsoft Exchange Vulnerability Exploits
Salt Typhoon has gained widespread attention in 2021 when they exploited a critical vulnerability
in Microsoft Exchange servers. This attack compromised multiple industries, including the
hospitality sector and various governmental organizations across multiple countries. The group
executed highly sophisticated techniques to exploit this zero-day vulnerability, using spear-
phishing and privilege escalation tactics to gain access to sensitive data.
2024 Presidential Election Interference
The group’s activities have escalated in severity, with the 2024 U.S. presidential election
providing an attractive target for their operations. Salt Typhoon reportedly compromised the
phone of President-elect Donald Trump and potentially Senator J.D. Vance’s device. This breach
allowed them to gather sensitive communications, including call metadata and personal
information, which could have far-reaching implications for political stability and national
security.

Behavior
Salt Typhoon is an advanced persistent threat (APT) group originating from the People’s Republic
of China. Believed to have been active since around 2020, they specialize in cyber-espionage
operations targeting critical sectors, government infrastructure, and private entities worldwide.
One of their most concerning ongoing campaigns involves the exploitation of vulnerabilities in the
American telecommunications network, which has placed national security and individual privacy
at significant risk.

Interestingly, the FBI and other cybersecurity organizations, which typically raise concerns about
encrypted messaging apps like Signal due to their potential use by criminals to evade
surveillance, have recently encouraged its adoption in specific scenarios. This shift stems from
the urgent need to secure communications in light of sophisticated cyber threats, such as those
posed by groups like Salt Typhoon. Signal’s robust end-to-end encryption makes it significantly
harder for malicious actors to intercept and exploit sensitive communications. For individuals and
organizations under constant threat of surveillance or data breaches, Signal provides a reliable
layer of protection against advanced persistent threats (APTs).
The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA), FBI, and NSA are collaborating to
neutralize Salt Typhoon's foothold in U.S. systems. The Federal Communications Commission
(FCC) recently released a document on December 5th, outlining new commitments and policy
recommendations aimed at reducing the likelihood of similar attacks in the future. This includes
strengthening encryption protocols, conducting regular penetration testing, and adopting zero-
trust architecture across critical systems.

Malware Analysis
Rootkit Attacks
Demodex is a chain of rootkits developed by the group known as ‘FamousSparrow’,
‘GhostEmporer’ or ‘SaltTyphoon’. The earliest mention of the demodex rootkit google occurs
around the 27th of November 2020, in a post by SecureBlink. The focus of the demodex rootkit
seems to be covert infiltration. Each iteration of Demodex improves on the last to avoid detection
on the victim machine. The rootkit disguises itself by resembling legitimate windows background
processes, such as using the name “WdiSystemhost” as opposed to the legitimate
“WdiSystemHost” when establishing connection to the command and control (C2) server.
Once the rootkit is on the victim machine and able to create and modify packets via the
“WdiSystemhost” process, it retrieves and loads a batch file to facilitate the install of its malware
payload. Once the batch file is available, it retrieves a cab file and runs the expand.exe script from
the batch file, thus installing the payload. Once “WdiSystemhost” mimics the valid registry key it
wires the service DLL into the system, it then runs PowerShell and launches the serviceDLL
which contains necessary functions for the payloads navigation in the victim machine.
Once demodex has implanted itself in the victim machine, it performs “living off the land”
operations by loading itself by using the open-source video game tool ‘Cheat Engine’ and hiding
behind its signature. Demodex's utilization of Cheat Engine, an open-source video game tool, is
particularly cunning because it takes advantage of the tool's reputation and functionality. Cheat
Engine is widely used by gamers to manipulate in-game data and is often granted elevated
privileges by users, making it an ideal cover for malicious software. By masquerading behind
Cheat Engine's signature, Demodex gains kernel-level access to the system, bypassing many
traditional security measures and allowing it to execute high-privilege operations without raising
suspicion. This strategy also complicates detection, as security tools may incorrectly associate
the rootkit's activity with legitimate Cheat Engine operations.

The rootkit's ability to exploit common Windows processes and tools really shows the
sophistication of Salt Typhoon's development team. The use of a misspelled but similar process
name, such as "WdiSystemhost," demonstrates how the group carefully crafts their malware to
blend into the victim's environment. Such subtle discrepancies are difficult for even advanced
monitoring systems to detect unless organizations meticulously log and analyze process activity.
This blending technique enables Demodex to establish persistent control over compromised
systems, often remaining undetected for months or even years.
SparrowDoor
Sparrow Door is an older piece of malware attributed to Salt Typhoon. First discovered in 2021,
its latest variant incorporates enhanced functionality, including clipboard logging, token
impersonation, and API hooking.
The loader is cleverly disguised as SearchIndexer.exe, another example of mimicking a legitimate
Windows process. It achieves execution through DLL side-loading, leveraging trusted software
like Notepad++'s updater. By embedding the malicious DLL libcurl.dll into the legitimate process,
SparrowDoor effectively evades detection by blending into the host environment.
Once deployed, SparrowDoor uses reflective loading to execute its backdoor payload, bypassing
traditional defenses like anti-virus software. The payload itself is XOR-encoded, an obfuscation
method that conceals its structure, making static analysis challenging. Additionally, the use of
hardcoded XOR keys to decode configuration data further complicates detection and analysis
efforts.
Persistence Mechanisms and Privilege Evasion of SparrowDoor
Persistence is achieved via* two primary methods:
1. Service Installation: The malware installs itself as a Windows service (SearchIndexer)
with an innocuous display name (Windows Searcher) and description. This method
ensures resilience, allowing the malware to restart with the system.
2. Registry Key Manipulation: If service installation fails, SparrowDoor falls back on adding
itself to the Run registry key, ensuring execution upon user login.
To remain inconspicuous, SparrowDoor impersonates the user token associated with
explorer.exe, a process that typically operates with standard privileges. This token

Vulnerability Breakdown
Salt Typhoon has demonstrated a strategic approach to cyber-espionage, leveraging known
vulnerabilities to infiltrate networks, gain persistence, and conduct long-term surveillance. Their
methodology combines exploiting unpatched systems with leveraging built-in administrative tools
and custom malware, enabling them to move laterally across networks while remaining
undetected. Below is a breakdown of specific vulnerabilities they have targeted and how they are
exploited to achieve their objectives.
Ivanti Connect Secure VPN Exploitation
•
CVE-2023-46805: This authentication bypass vulnerability in Ivanti Connect Secure (ICS)
allows attackers to bypass security controls and gain unauthorized access to the system.
By exploiting flaws in the authentication mechanism, Salt Typhoon can establish a
foothold in the target network without requiring valid credentials.
• CVE-2024-21887: This command injection vulnerability affects authenticated
administrators, enabling attackers to execute arbitrary commands directly on the
appliance. Salt Typhoon uses this vulnerability to escalate privileges and deploy
additional malware or scripts to solidify their control over the compromised VPN gateway.
How It’s Used: VPN gateways are critical entry points for remote workers. By exploiting these
vulnerabilities, Salt Typhoon can intercept sensitive traffic, harvest credentials, and establish a
persistent connection to the target’s internal network. Once inside, they pivot to other systems by
leveraging the VPN’s trust relationship with internal resources.
Fortinet FortiClient EMS SQL Injection
• CVE-2023-48788: This SQL injection vulnerability in Fortinet’s Enterprise Management
Server (EMS) allows attackers to inject malicious SQL commands through improperly
sanitized inputs. Exploiting this vulnerability can give Salt Typhoon direct access to the
EMS database, where sensitive information such as user credentials, configuration data,
and system logs is stored.
How It’s Used: After breaching the EMS database, Salt Typhoon can manipulate configurations,
extract sensitive data, and even use compromised credentials to infiltrate other devices managed
by the EMS. This access enables lateral movement and the deployment of additional payloads
across the network.
Sophos Firewall Code Injection
• CVE-2022-3236: This vulnerability allows attackers to inject malicious code into the User
Portal and Webadmin components of the Sophos Firewall. Remote code execution (RCE)
is possible, giving the attacker full control over the device.
How It’s Used: By compromising the firewall, Salt Typhoon can disable security measures,
create backdoors, and monitor or manipulate network traffic. The control over the firewall serves
as a launchpad for further attacks, enabling the group to expand their reach within the target
network while masking malicious activity as legitimate traffic.
Microsoft Exchange Chained ProxyLogon
•
CVE-2021-26855: A server-side request forgery (SSRF) vulnerability that allows
unauthorized attackers to send arbitrary HTTP requests.
CVE-2021-26857: A vulnerability that enables attackers to execute code in the context of
the Exchange server.
CVE-2021-26858 and CVE-2021-27065: Both allow attackers to write arbitrary files to
the system, enabling further exploitation and backdoor creation.

Salt Typhoon’s exploitation of these vulnerabilities shows a recurring theme: targeting edge
devices and high-value internal servers to gain entry, maintain persistence, and achieve
espionage objectives. These vulnerabilities are typically exploited in environments where
patching is delayed or security configurations are lax. By targeting devices like VPNs, firewalls,
and Exchange servers, Salt Typhoon can bypass external defenses and directly access valuable
data or critical systems.
Once inside, Salt Typhoon leverages legitimate tools like PowerShell, WMI, and PsExec to blend
in with normal administrative activity. This, combined with the use of custom malware like
SparrowDoor, allows them to move laterally, escalate privileges, and establish long-term access
for espionage purposes. Their operations often go unnoticed for months or even years, making
them a formidable threat to organizations with inadequate patching or monitoring practices.
